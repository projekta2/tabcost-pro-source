📌 What is TabCost?

TabCost turns your inactive tabs into a real economic metric. It's not just a tab manager: it's an attention auditor that assigns a monetary value to the time you lose on abandoned tasks or distractions.

It helps you to:

  • ✅ Become aware of how much money "slips away" with forgotten tabs.
  • ✅ Automatically close what you don't use (Pro option).
  • ✅ Prioritize your focus where it really matters.
  • ✅ Export the list of inactive tabs to CSV (Pro) to analyze your habits.

🎯 Who is it for?

  • Freelancers and self-employed who charge by the hour and want to minimize distractions.
  • Students and professionals who need to maintain concentration.
  • Anyone who wants to understand the "opportunity cost" of having 20 tabs open while working on a single one.
  • Remote teams looking to improve collective productivity (aggregated metrics coming soon).

Available in Free (basic features) and Pro (advanced features via one‑time purchase).

⚙️ How is the cost calculated?

The formula is very simple:

Cost per minute = (Hourly rate ÷ 60) × Impact percentage

  • Hourly rate: you set it (e.g., $50/h).
  • Impact percentage: default 20% (adjustable in Pro). It represents the realistic opportunity cost of an idle tab (not 100% of your time, because you are not 100% unproductive).

Example: Rate $50/h → $0.833/min → with 20% impact → $0.166 per minute of inactive tab. If you have 5 inactive tabs for 1 hour, the accumulated cost is ≈$0.83. Per day it could be $5‑10, per month more than $150 "lost" in opportunities.

TabCost only counts tabs that have been inactive beyond the grace period (default 5 minutes, adjustable in Pro). The active tab never generates cost.

🕒 Detailed behavior: why the counter sometimes "stops"

It's normal to see that the cost does not increase during long periods without interaction. This is not a bug, but a design feature of Chrome/Edge to save resources.

🔁 Why does the counter stop?

Short answer: Chrome suspends the extension when you don't use it to save battery and CPU.

Technical explanation: TabCost uses a service worker. If there is no interaction (you don't open the popup, you don't change tabs, you don't touch the browser), Chrome may stop it after a few seconds. When you interact again, the extension "wakes up" and recovers lost time, but only up to the last 15 minutes (default limit).

👉 So, if you leave your computer for 2 hours without touching anything, when you return only the last 15 minutes of inactivity will be counted. This is intentional to avoid background resource consumption.

⏱️ Why does the active tab never generate cost?

Because the extension understands that if you are looking at a tab, it is not "inactive". Only tabs that have not been visited beyond the grace period are penalized.

🧠 Why does a tab that was not counting start again after being abandoned?

When you click on a tab (or reload it), TabCost records that moment as the last interaction. If you then leave it, it will start counting from zero after the grace minutes. This is expected behavior and ensures that actively used tabs are not penalized.

✅ Conclusion:

TabCost is accurate during normal activity (tab switching, browser usage). Only during prolonged inactivity does it apply the 15‑minute limit to protect performance. This is the standard way Chrome manages extensions.

🕒 Comportamiento detallado: por qué a veces "se para" el contador

Es normal observar que el coste no aumenta durante largos periodos sin interacción. Esto no es un error, sino una característica del diseño de Chrome/Edge para ahorrar recursos.

🔁 ¿Por qué se detiene el contador?

Respuesta corta: Chrome suspende la extensión cuando no la usas para ahorrar batería y CPU.

Explicación técnica: TabCost usa un service worker. Si no hay interacción (no abres el popup, no cambias de pestaña, no tocas el navegador), Chrome puede detenerlo a los pocos segundos. Cuando vuelves a interactuar, la extensión “despierta” y recupera el tiempo perdido, pero solo hasta los últimos 15 minutos (límite por defecto).

👉 Por tanto, si dejas el ordenador 2 horas sin tocar nada, al volver solo se sumarán costes por los últimos 15 minutos de inactividad. Esto es intencionado para evitar consumir recursos en segundo plano.

⏱️ ¿Por qué la pestaña activa nunca genera coste?

Porque la extensión entiende que si estás mirando una pestaña, no es “inactiva”. Solo penaliza las que llevan sin ser visitadas más allá del período de gracia.

🧠 ¿Por qué al volver a una pestaña que ya no contaba, vuelve a sumar después de abandonarla?

Al hacer clic en una pestaña (o recargarla), TabCost registra ese momento como la última interacción. Si luego la dejas, empezará a contar desde cero tras los minutos de gracia. Es el comportamiento esperado y garantiza que las pestañas que usas activamente no se penalicen.

✅ Conclusión:

TabCost es preciso durante periodos de actividad normal (cambios de pestaña, uso del navegador). Solo en inactividad prolongada aplica el límite de 15 minutos para proteger el rendimiento. Es la forma estándar en que Chrome gestiona las extensiones.
